René Descartes was a 17th-century French philosopher and mathematician, often called the father of modern philosophy. Born into a world where science and religion often clashed, Descartes was fascinated by the question of certainty—how can we truly know anything? He wanted to build knowledge on a foundation that was absolutely certain, something that could not be doubted. This led him to embrace a method of radical skepticism: he decided to question everything, even the existence of the world around him and his own body.

In this process of deep doubt, Descartes arrived at a striking realization: while he could doubt the existence of everything else, he could not doubt that he was doubting, thinking, or questioning. This led him to his famous declaration: Cogito, ergo sum—“I think, therefore I am.” Descartes claimed that the very act of thinking proved his existence. Even if a powerful deceiver tricked him about the external world, the fact that he was aware of being deceived confirmed that there was a thinking self at the core of his experience.
